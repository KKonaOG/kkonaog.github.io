Introduction

My name is Kyler Farrar, and I am majoring in Computer Engineering at Mississippi State University! As part of ECE 4723 (Embedded Systems), we are tasked to perform a dissection of an electronic product. The product I chose to dissect is the Tiny Touch Tablet by VTech Industries. Product Information

# Product History

VTech is regarded as one of the largest manufacturers of retail cordless telephones in both the commercial and residential fields. Founded in 1976 by Chi-Yun and Stephen Leung, the companies initial interests involved video game development, even releasing a TV game console version of Pong. After minor success in the video game industry, VTech started to move into electronic learning products in February of 1980 with a product called the Lesson One. The goal of Lesson One was to teach children the basics of spelling and mathematics. The following years involved VTechs investigations in other markets of potential profits, even the personal computer market. In 1985, VTech began the development on the first iterations of a cordless telephone, which was later introduced in 1991 as the world’s first fully digital cordless telephone. VTech’s primary industries, as a result, are in cordless telephones (and related technologies/accessories) and electronic learning products. An interesting anecdote is that VTech was among the first businesses to attempt to tackle the concept of electronic learning products.

# Trackball Rotation Output Waveform

The trackball assembly consists of a metal circle and several thin/flexible copper filaments. These filaments generate voltages on the metal circle when the trackball is rotated. The MCU detects this and plays the appropriate sound, depending on which assembly is generating the change in voltage.

# Button Waveform (Unpressed)

Due to the wiring situation, I could not demonstrate a button press and also record the wavefrom data. These buttons work by shorting two contacts together when the button is pressed. Thusly the waveform you see below will become a uniform voltage (near 0V) when pressed. The MCU detects this (after some debouncing) and plays the appropriate sound for the button.

Lessons Learned

Overall, I learned a LOT from this project. Probably the biggest skill advancements were that in PCB analysis techniques and increased proficiency in KiCad (the schematic creation software). One more interesting fact I learned that it is not uncommon to reuuse PCBs in multiple products. I also learned that companies will use 0 Ohm resistors in place of traces in order to decrease the complexity of routing! The biggest take away I have had, is that, even though a product seems extremely straight forward, it can still be suprisingly complex in its implementation. I imagine the reason the schematic/PCB design for this product was so complex was to cut costs and maximize productivity.
